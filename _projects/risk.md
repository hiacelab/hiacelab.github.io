This research direction studies how drivers perceive risk, adapt their behavior, and respond to changing traffic and automation conditions. The goal is to better understand how human perception, cognition, and behavior interact with intelligent transportation systems and automated driving technologies.

The project includes work on traffic density transitions, driver responses to dynamic environments, and the needs and perceptions of diverse user populations in automated mobility contexts. It also reflects the lab’s broader interest in human factors, behavioral modeling, and data-driven design for safer and more effective transportation systems.

Related publications include **“Asymmetric Shifts in Risk Perception: Evaluating Driver Responses to Traffic Density Transitions”.
